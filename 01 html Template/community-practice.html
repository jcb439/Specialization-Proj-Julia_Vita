STATE OF KNOWLEDGE
Julia Beitel & Vita Sipayung

Research across design, sociology, and environmental studies shows that environmental participation often takes shape through everyday practices rather than big declarations. What counts as care is being redefined in small but politically meaningful acts that gain significance when enacted through material practices rather than verbal commitments alone.

Given this, we aim to explore ways of incentivizing individual actions that advance sustainability within the “middle space” — bridging large-scale, financially driven frameworks and isolated, altruistic habits.

Everyday Material Practices Matter

Repair, composting, food sharing, and gardening are powerful ways people express environmental care beyond words. For example, community composting in New York City connects household waste practices to broader urban systems (Morrow & Davies, 2021).

Invisible Political Acts Can Be Politically Powerful

Minimal, everyday gestures—such as cleaning, healing, or maintaining domestic spaces—are often overlooked but carry transformative significance in sustaining life in toxic environments (Tironi, 2018; Ourabah, 2025).

Connecting Individuals to Systems

Citizen sensing projects, such as DIY air pollution monitoring, demonstrate how local data collection can influence policy and infrastructure, making small gestures part of systemic change (Gabrys, 2017; Knox, 2021).

Frameworks of Care

Much of this work draws on the ethics of care and more-than-human approaches, emphasizing interdependence across people, communities, and environments (Sovová et al., 2021).

Sustaining Participation

Research suggests that reflection, community networks, and skill-sharing are key to keeping care practices alive. While financial incentives play a role, cultural recognition of "unspoken" acts like repair, reuse, and composting may be just as important (Isenhour & Reno, 2019; Graziano & Trogal, 2017; Young & Rosner, 2018).
