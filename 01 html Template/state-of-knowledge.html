State of Knowledge

We already have many systems to track and incentivize climate action, ranging from macro-level frameworks (UN Sustainable Development Goals, Climate Action Tracker, carbon credits) to everyday consumer apps (Too Good To Go, Recyclebank, FoodWise). These approaches tend to live at two extremes: either monitoring nations and corporations, or encouraging isolated individual habits.

What's missing is the middle space: tools that connect daily practices with collective accountability, making small actions visible as meaningful forms of civic participation. This gap is especially important in urban contexts like New York City, where individuals, small businesses, and local policies overlap.

Examples at the Consumer & Small Business Level

• NYC Bottle Bill – monetary refunds for recycling bottles and cans.

• Targeted Discounts – e.g., save money at a café if you bring your own mug.

• Recyclebank – earn redeemable points for eco-friendly actions.

• EarthRewards – a currency tied to emission reductions.

• FoodWise app – log and reduce food waste behaviors.

• Amazon Packaging Incentives – discounts for choosing less packaging.

Market & Policy Incentives

• U.S. Residential Clean Energy Credit – 30% tax credit for installing solar, wind, or battery storage through 2032.

• Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit – annual tax credit of up to $3,200 for qualifying upgrades.

• Carbon Taxes / Cap-and-Trade – place a financial cost on emissions, incentivizing companies to reduce pollution.

• Tradeable Carbon Credits – organizations can sell excess emission reductions to those who need offsets.
